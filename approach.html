The Four Pillars Framework

It can feel impossible to keep up with the pace of generative AI tools, models, and features. I made an early decision to look for the parts of using chatbots that won't change tomorrow or in ten years. I tested, observed, reflected, and iterated until I came up with what I call the Four Pillars Framework. You can find it freely below, because this is 101 level knowledge you should have access to. People who know more about AI than me might consider the language I use oversimplified, but I've found that learners don't need jargon or technical specifics to start using chatbots more effectively.

Pillar 1. Assumptions

When you send the AI a message, it considers what you've said in the context of what you've said so far, and then makes assumptions about what you actually want to happen. This process is happening with every message you send, and it's easiest to understand when you see it happening with an image generator like Google Gemini:

View full example ↗

I instructed Gemini to "Create a window." I didn't say what kind or in what setting, but it filled in the gaps with a background, time of day, type of window, and a whole lot more. Without any additional information, it's making assumptions about what the everyday person might expect to see.

Pillar 1 is operating at all times, like when you say, "Make this resume better." The AI doesn't know what "better" means to you, so it draws on its knowledge base to predict what someone would typically expect to see happen. This is how you end up with very confident results that focus on the wrong thing. The first move you can make is replacing ambiguous words like "better" with clearly measurable definitions.

Pillar 2. A Complete Story

Knowing that Pillar 1 is always operating means you can guide how it works. Turn assumptions into educated guesses by telling a complete stroy. Define your problem and what you want the AI to do about it. Here's that window example again:

View full example ↗

I gave Gemini a little bit more background on why I need the image and what it needs to do. Those signals guided it to make more educated guesses, like selecting an outdoor winter scene. But because Pillar 1 is always happening, it filled in gaps by choosing a primarily white audience and a Christmas scene. If what it fills in doesn't work for your needs, then you might not have shared a complete enough story.

With something like resume writing, it can be harder to spot the nuances resulting from effective and ineffective storytelling. Describe what the resume is for, provide the job description, explain what you think fails now, and highlight the elements you want to preserve. What you share matters, so giving irrelevant information or sharing too much can be distracting. Finding the goldilocks zone where it's not too much or too little takes practice and reflection on what typically works for your style.

Pillar 3. Questions

You could draft pages of storytelling and still never touch on the parts of your story the AI needs to know to make the right assumptions. Explain what you think is relevant and then invite the AI to interview you by asking one question at a time to surface the most informative parts of your situation. Back to Gemini:

View full example ↗

I instructed Gemini to ask me 3 questions one at a time until it could handle the parts I didn't explain. (I usually don't set a question limit so that it can gather as much relevant information as needed.) After answering, it automatically triggered the image generator when the questions ended (which not all tools will do). The resulting scene is nondenominational, used location data to assume a relevant high school name, and includes a very white audience.

When you're tailoring a resume or being interviewed about anything else, you'll get closer to what you're envisioning with each response you give. No matter how many you answer, Pillar 1 continues to operate. Here, your audience being primarily non-white might not come up. In job applications, you might never see a question about your international status or another topic important to you, so the AI will almost always make assumptions that don't apply to you. Always reflect on what's happened so far in this conversation and across others, and start frontloading the parts that matter.

Pillar 4. Rough Draft

Define your terms. Tell your story. Interview with the AI endlessly. You'll never cover every detail about you, and that shouldn't be the goal anyways. The goal is always to help the AI make better educated guesses about what you want to happen with the important parts of what you haven't said yet.

For this reason, treat every interaction with the AI like it's a rough draft. Not just the final output, but every response you get. Inspect what it says. Think about it. Call out what's wrong. Learn to start a new conversation when it gets stuck on a mistake, and strive toward knowing when what you've created is good enough to move from AI to a more traditional means of finalizing the draft.

Using AI effectively means not treating it like an easy button. Looking at each response as a rough draft means this work can take just as long or longer than doing it the traditional way, which means this might not be an approach for everybody. But if you adapt it, you'll think deeper and anticipate more problems, so that whatever you do create holds up to scrutiny better than what you might have created on your own.

Please share this page with anyone who wants to start using AI more effectively. The Four Pillars represent a way of thinking that works with any chatbot. What it takes to build truly effective prompts that work with your choice of tool takes time, practice, and reflection, and connecting with others in your field to share ideas. I'm here to help with any step along the way.

A note on expertise

I'm not an AI expert. I'm a communication coach. I've spent a few years now talking to generative AI chatbots like Claude, Gemini, and ChatGPT. I get excited about psychology, interpretation, and influence, which are thinking skills that transfer well into this space. But that committed interest doesn't make me an expert. There's plenty I haven't explored yet, like the effective ways to deploy agents or specialized tools beyond the frontier models. Be cautious with anyone who says they're an expert who isn't trained in coding, machine learning, or artifical intelligence, especially if they're trying to sell you "the right way" to use these tools.
